The Menu (2022)

★★★★★★★★★★ (10/10)

Released November 18, 2022
Runtime 1h 47m
Director(s) Mark Mylod
Writer(s) Agatha Christie (original concept), Seth Reiss, Will Tracy
Country United States
Language(s) Spanish; Castilian, English
Studio(s) Gary Sanchez Productions, TSG Entertainment, Hyperobject Industries, Searchlight Pictures
Genre(s) Horror, Thriller, Comedy

A young couple travels to a remote island to eat at an exclusive restaurant where the chef has prepared a lavish menu, with some shocking surprises.

Review

*SPOILERS AHEAD*

The Menu is a dark, thrilling, and surprisingly funny satire that manages to be both horrifying and thought-provoking. It mixes psychological tension with biting humor, creating a story that's both stylish and unsettling.

The cinematography is beautiful, giving every scene a sense of precision and control that mirrors the restaurant itself. The cast is excellent across the board. Each actor completely sells their role. You quickly learn to hate Tyler for his arrogance, while Chef Slowik's presence dominates every moment he's on screen. His calm intensity, commanding claps, and unwavering charisma make him one of the most magnetic antagonists I've seen in any film.

Despite his cruelty, Chef Slowik is disturbingly relatable. His frustration with entitled guests and his faded passion for the art of cooking make him feel human, not just villainous. His philosophy is perfectly summed up in one line to Tyler: "You are why the mystery has been drained from our art." This quote encapsulates his motive of punishing those who cheapen his craft and highlighting the consequences of entitlement and pretension. The moment he allows Margot to leave the island is one of the film's highlights. Not because he softens, but because of her genuine appreciation for his craft reignites his buried passion. Watching him smile as he makes her cheeseburger feels oddly touching and wholesome.

The film's tension explodes in the shocking scene where Jeremy kills himself and transforms the dinner from pretentious luxury into controlled chaos. From there, The Menu becomes a relentless mix of satire and psychological horror, balancing dread with dark humor.

The dialogue is razor-sharp, packed with quotable lines and subtle mockery of the rich and self-absorbed. Scenes like the "no bread" moment or the tortillas revealing the guests' secrets are both hilarious and chilling.

By the end, I was fully invested and genuinely glad Margot survived. She was never part of the system Chef Slowik wanted to destroy, and seeing her take that satisfying bite of her cheeseburger made for the perfect ending.

Honestly, I found The Menu to be flawless. The pacing, tone, story, cinematography, and performances all came together perfectly. Thrilling, horrifying, dark, and wickedly funny, it's a perfectly plated (no pun intended) blend of satire and suspense that I absolutely loved.
