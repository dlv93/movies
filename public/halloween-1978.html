Genre(s) Horror, Thriller

Fifteen years after murdering his sister on Halloween night 1963, Michael Myers escapes from a mental hospital and returns to the small town of Haddonfield, Illinois to kill again.

Review

*SPOILERS AHEAD*

I didn't find Halloween particulary scary or very engaging, but I did enjoy some aspects. Michael Myers isn't so much a character as he is a concept. He is silent, robotic, and relentless, killing without motive, reaction, or personality, which makes it hard to connect with the threat he represents. Unlike other killers who interact with their victims by taunting them, playing with them, or reacting to their fear, Michael feels like a force of nature. Even if the goal is to embody pure evil, it would probably work better for me if the film showed more gore or creative kills to make his presence feel more intense and threatening.

I liked Michael's look. The mask, the oufit, and the way he physically movies make him visually memorable. He also shows tiny moments of goofiness, like when he's cruising near Laurie, Annie, and Lynda. Lynda yells "Hey, jerk! Speed kills, and Michael slams on the brakes and just sits in the middle of the street, almost as if she had cursed the name of his mother. Another moment is later, in the bedroom scene with Lynda, he walks in wearing a bedsheet and Bob's glasses over it. These brief glimpses of humor are amusing, but they still don't give him much personality.

The chase scenes with Laurie are the closest the movie comes to being consistently entertaining. Some of it is overdramatic, and her constant whimpering can be a bit grating, but I'll admit these moments are the most engaging part of the film. One element that does consistently work for me is the iconic Michael Myers theme. The music really adds tension and atmosphere, and it helps make the scenes feel ominous even when the killer himself doesn't inspire much fear.

I can appreciate the historical importance of Halloween. It was one of the first films of its kind and clearly influenced the slasher genre in ways we still see today. I respect that and understand why it's considered innovative, even though, as a modern viewer, it doesn't fully engage me emotionally or scare me.

Overall, it's a technically competent and ocassionally entertaining film, but as a modern viewer looking for visceral tension or emotional engagement, it just doesn't hit the mark for me.
