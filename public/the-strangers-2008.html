The Strangers (2008)

★★★★★★★☆☆☆ (7/10)

Released May 30, 2008
Runtime 1h 26m
Director(s) Bryan Bertino
Writer(s) Bryan Bertino (screenplay)
Country United States
Language(s) English
Studio(s) Intrepid Pictures, Rogue Pictures, Vertigo Entertainment, Mandate Pictures
Genre(s) Horror, Thriller

After a 4 a.m. knock at the door and haunting voices, Kristen McKay and James Hoyt's remote getaway becomes a psychological night of terror as three masked strangers invade. Now they must go far beyond what they thought themselves capable of if they hope to survive.

Review

The Strangers is a solid horror movie that shows how much terror can come from sound alone. It builds so much tension through simple noises like knocks, creaks, footsteps, and moments of silence. One of my favorite examples is when Merle Haggard's Mama Tried plays on the vinyl while Mike walks through the house, unaware that the burlap-masked stranger is silently following behind him. The country song paired with the quiet dread of that moment works perfectly.

What makes the film so unsettling isn't violence, it's the pursuit. The strangers are never in a rush; they toy with their victims, stretching out the fear until it feels unbearable. There's something deeply disturbing about how casual and random their cruelty is, like they're doing it just because they can. This is made even worse when, if asked why they're doing it, they respond with a cold, emotionless "Because you were home." That simple, matter-of-fact answer makes them feel utterly evil. There's no motive beyond pure, senseless malice. The contrast between their happy masks and their emotionless behavior makes it even creepier. The burlap-sack mask with the mouth line, especially, was my favorite. The stranger with this mask's first scene, when he steps from around the corner behind Kristen in the kitchen, is easily one of, if not the most, film's most iconic moments.

There isn't much of a story here, but that actually fits the type of horror it's going for. It's more about atmosphere and psychological torment than plot. That said, I never felt much connection to Kristen. After she rejects James' proposal, it's hard to sympathize with her, and the movie doesn't really do much to rebuild that connection. James, despite being my favorite of the two, makes a few unrealistic decisions. Another thing that didn't work for me were the slow-motion shots scattered throughout.

Overall though, The Strangers works. It's tense, eerie, and grounded, with some moments that stick in your mind after the credits roll. It's not perfect, but for what it set out to be which is a simple, psychological home-invasion thriller, I think it nails it.
