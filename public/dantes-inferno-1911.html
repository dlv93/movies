Dante's Inferno (1911)

★★★★★☆☆☆☆☆ (5/10)

Released March 11, 1911
Runtime 1h 12m
Director(s) Giuseppe de Liguoro, Francesco Bertolini, Adolfo Padovan
Writer(s) Dante Alighieri (original story)
Country Italy
Language(s) Italian
Studio(s) Milano Film
Genre(s) Fantasy, Horror
Original Title L'Inferno

The classic tale of Dante's journey through Hell, loosely adapted from the Divine Comedy and inspired by the illustrations of Gustav Doré. This historically important film stands as the first feature from Italy and the oldest fully-surviving feature in the world, and boasts beautiful sets and special effects that stand above other cinema of the era.

Review

I can definitely see why this film is historically important. The filmmakers clearly put great effort into the wardrobe, set, and especially the special effects, considering the era. The story adapts the Inferno section of Dante's Divine Comedy, showing Dante and Virgil touring Hell and witnessing various sinners' punishments.

Visually, the film looks old, but in a way that almost works in its favor, adding to the horror. Being a silent film is understandable for 1911, and while some moments are naturally unclear due to the format, I personally find silent films difficult to engage with. There are many historical and mythological figures I didn't recognize, which I imagine would confuse most modern viewers without background knowledge of Dante's era, classical myths, or Italian history.

Outside of that, the journey through Hell with Dante and Virgil is portrayed fairly well, and I liked most of the demon designs. That said, I was disappointed with Lucifer, who didn't look as impressive as I expected. There are also a few unintentionally funny moments. In particular, the scene where Dante's path to the hill of salvation is blocked by a she-wolf, a lion, and a panther. The she-wolf is literally just a regular dog, which I found amusing.

Overall, the film works better as a piece of film history than as something entertaining to watch today.
